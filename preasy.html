Preasy - A UX Case Study on Rethinking Presentation Preparation

Preasy is a web app which helps users efficiently create slides, rehearse presentations with feedback, and manage team workflows. It supports students and professionals who struggle with clear and confident presentations due to unpreparedness and poor structure.

Problem

People rely on too many scattered tools when preparing for a presentation—Google Slides,Canva, chat apps, Zoom, Microsoft Powerpoint, and more. This disjointed process increases stress and leads to last-minute chaos. Users lack a unified space to plan, rehearse, gather feedback, and work with others efficiently.

Problem Statement:Presentation preparation is rarely streamlined.Students and professionals jump between tools for design, rehearsal, feedback, and collaboration—causing friction, confusion, and stress.

Objective:Research, plan, and design a web experience to help users prepare presentations more efficiently, with a focus on reducing prep time and improving delivery confidence through a unified workflow.

Research & Insights

We wanted to understand how individuals prepare for presentations—what slows them down, what tools they use, and what support they need but lack.

  • Competitive Analysis: Most existing solutions have speech analysis & AI generation features, but they lack interactive Q&A, and collaborative planning tools.
  • 10+ Survey Responses: Users struggled with condensing ideas, collecting resources, and engaging their audiences.
  • 4 User Interviews: People felt overwhelmed, struggled with teamwork and wanted better pacing tools and feedback structure.

    • Faced with diverse presentation habits and workflows, we needed to identify which moments in the preparation journey mattered most. By clustering qualitative insights through affinity mapping, we uncovered 3 recurring pain points: the need for effective feedback during Q&A, smoother collaboration management, and clearer support for structured storytelling. These insights guided our user personas, journey mapping, and ultimately informed the prioritization of core features.

    User Persona & Journey Map

    To clarify our direction, we created a user persona based on research data. Neve, a graduate student who frequently presents design and research work, became a key reference. While she is confident in her content and visual skills, she struggles with pacing, handling off-topic Q&A, and managing team collaboration.


    To map out the full experience, we created a user journey map detailing each stage of preparing, rehearsing, and delivering a presentation. It was built early in the process and originally leaned heavily on AI-driven solutions. Although our product direction evolved significantly, the journey map still provided clarity around key moments of friction—especially during planning, group coordination, and live delivery.

    Ideation & Strategy

    With a clearer understanding of user behaviors, pain points, and moments of friction, I transitioned from research to defining the product's core values. These principles distilled our research insights into clear design intentions—and served as the foundation for ideation and feature development.

  • Clarity: Simplify storytelling process to help users deliver clear and structured presentation.
  • Confidence Building: Empower users with iterative feedback and adaptive simulation to build confidence.
  • Seamless Collaboration:Ensure smooth coordination and consistency through real-time transparent communication.

    • To bridge the gap between design principles and concrete functionality, I created an opportunity sheet with my HMW statement. This mapped user needs and pain points against potential features, organized by phase and aligned with our core values.

    It helped me evaluate which ideas offered the most impact, identify feasibility constraints, and prioritize solutions that would best support clarity, confidence, and collaboration throughout the presentation process. This strategic exercise directly informed the core features that Preasy would ultimately offer.

    Core Features

    • Practice Mode with timer and rehearsal feedback
    • Feedback Tags to ask for help on specific issues like pacing
    • Team Dashboard to assign slides and track progress

    Branding & Visual Identity

    Branding Moodboard Experiments

    Preasy's visual design uses clean layout grids, soft neutral tones, and a balance of utility and friendliness. The logo and font choices aim to convey approachability without sacrificing focus. I designed a Figma-based system with reusable components and scalable text styles.

    User Testing

    After usability testing with classmates, peers and professors, I received feedback on hierarchy clarity, button size, and onboarding cues. Iterative refinement improved discoverability and supported different workflows.

    Reflection

    This was the first time I led a UX project through a full cycle—from research to case study documentation. I improved in narrowing scope, prioritizing features, and presenting insights clearly. Seeing how real feedback shaped the product was exciting and humbling. Preasy confirmed my passion for UI/UX digital product design and helped me grow into a more intentional designer.
